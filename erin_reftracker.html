⚠  Most grad programs require 2–3 letters. Federal positions often need 3. Ask at least 4–6 weeks in advance — earlier is always better. Faculty receive many requests in fall; reach out in August–September before the rush.
📝 Who to ask▶

The strongest letters for environmental science grad programs come from people who have seen your scientific work directly. In rough priority order:

  • Your capstone advisor / GEOL 495 supervisor — can speak directly to your Pigeon River research, field methods, and independent thinking. This is your strongest letter for any wetland, water quality, or contaminated-site program.
  • Lab / field course instructors (GEOL 455 Wetlands, GEOL 423 Contaminated Rivers, ENVH 310 Water Quality, NRM 479 Mammalogy) — these professors saw your hands-on skills and know your technical depth.
  • Research or internship supervisors — anyone who supervised you in a professional or field context can speak to work ethic, reliability, and applied skills.
  • Academic advisor or department chair — good for a third letter if you need one, attesting to overall academic record and character.

⚠ Avoid asking professors from large lecture courses who don’t know you personally — a generic letter hurts more than it helps.

✉ How to ask — the right way▶

Send a personal email. Include:

  • A specific reminder of your work together — the course, the project, a result they’d remember. Don’t assume they remember you clearly.
  • What programs you’re applying to — list them with deadlines. A table works well: Program | Deadline | Submission method.
  • What you’d like them to highlight — give them a hook specific to what they know about you. “I’d love it if you could speak to my fieldwork in the Pigeon River watershed and my ability to design and execute independent research.”
  • Your resume and SOP draft — attach both so they have everything without chasing you.
  • Submission instructions for each program — some email the recommender directly; others use a portal. Clarify this upfront.
  • An explicit out — say “Please let me know if you’re not able to commit to this timeline — I completely understand.” This makes it easy to decline gracefully, and you’ll get a better letter from someone who genuinely said yes.
📅 Timing strategy▶
  • August–September 2026: Reach out to all recommenders before faculty are overwhelmed with fall requests. Give them the full program list and all deadlines at this point.
  • 2–3 weeks before each deadline: Send a friendly reminder with the specific program name, deadline, and submission link. Don’t wait until the week of.
  • After submission: Send a thank-you email. Tell them the outcome when you hear back — faculty genuinely appreciate knowing how things turned out.
  • If a deadline passes without confirmation: Check the portal first. Then follow up politely: “Just checking in to make sure the letter for [Program] went through — the deadline was [date].”
📄 Materials packet to send them▶

Put together a single organized email or shared folder with:

  • Your current CV / resume
  • A draft statement of purpose (even if not final — it helps them tailor the letter)
  • A program list with deadlines — one table, all programs, sorted by deadline date
  • A brief “what I’d like you to highlight” paragraph specific to this recommender’s knowledge of you
  • Submission instructions for each program (portal link, direct email, etc.)
  • Your unofficial transcript if they want context on your full coursework record

Make it as easy as possible for them. The more organized your packet, the better and faster the letter.

💡 Erin-specific tips▶
  • Your Pigeon River / Hurricane Helene capstone is your strongest asset — ensure at least one recommender can speak to this project specifically: the field methods, the contamination findings, and your independent initiative.
  • For NC State / Dr. Forrester’s lab: if you have already emailed Dr. Forrester and received a response, mention that in your ask to your recommender — it signals that this program is realistic and helps them frame the letter.
  • For federal positions (USGS, FWS, USACE): a supervisor-level reference who can speak to field skills and reliability carries the most weight. Academic letters are secondary for these roles.
  • If asking a professor you haven’t been in touch with since graduation: re-introduce yourself clearly — your name, the course, the semester, and a specific memory they’d have of you. Professors teach hundreds of students each year; help them place you.
  • WCU faculty who wrote your undergraduate recommendations (if any) are the warmest ask — they already know you well and want to support your next step.
