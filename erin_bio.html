Competitive standing
What makes this profile competitive

Erin's combination of hands-on field skills and strong academic credentials puts her in a solid position for funded MS programs. A 3.731 GPA with Dean's and Chancellor's List recognition is above the typical minimum (3.0–3.2) for research assistantships at R1 universities.

The Hurricane Helene lead contamination project — analyzing Pigeon River Basin sediments after a major hurricane — is a standout research experience that directly maps to faculty working on water quality, contaminant transport, and disaster ecology. This should anchor any statement of purpose or cover letter.

GIS proficiency (both basic and advanced) is in high demand for ecological and environmental research, especially in programs involving remote sensing, spatial analysis, or landscape ecology.

Her environmental justice coursework in the Appalachian context — unusual for an undergraduate — gives her a policy and community dimension that purely technical candidates lack. Combined with AmeriCorps service and Hurricane Helene relief volunteering, she presents as both a scholar and a committed community member.

Application strategy

Funded MS spots are often filled by students who contact professors before applying. A short, targeted email to a professor whose research aligns — especially in wetlands, water quality, or wildlife ecology — is the most effective first step. Many funded positions are never publicly posted.

The Pigeon River/Hurricane Helene research and the relief volunteering should be mentioned together in personal statements — the overlap between scholarship and service in the same event is a compelling narrative. The Clemson University Hurricane Helene forest recovery MS position is a near-perfect thematic match.

One honest gap: only one formal statistics course (MATH 270, B+). Address this by emphasizing the quantitative nature of GIS suitability analysis and the geomorphological-geochemical methods in GEOL 423. Consider noting openness to additional methods coursework in graduate programs.

WCU program structure
BS Environmental Science (Interdisciplinary)
WCU's interdisciplinary BS in Environmental Science requires 66–69 major hours built around a core of biology, chemistry, geology, and geospatial analysis, plus four ES-specific courses (ES 150, 250, 350, 495). Six category requirements allow specialization; upper-level program electives are open to any relevant course across the university. Total degree: 120 hours.
Erin's six category selections:
Environmental Health → Water Quality Control (ENVH 310)
Environmental Policy → International Environmental Politics (PSC 354)
Environment & Society → Environmental & Animal Ethics (PAR 333)
Statistical Methods → Statistical Methods I (MATH 270)
Advanced Field Science → Intro Fish & Wildlife Ecology & Management (NRM 330)
Environmental Analysis → Wetlands (GEOL 455)
